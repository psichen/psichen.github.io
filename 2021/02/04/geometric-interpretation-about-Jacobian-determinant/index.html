Geometric interpretation about Jacobian determinant

Posted on 2021-02-04 Edited on 2021-02-05 Views:

In scientific computing, integration by substitution is a very common skill. For example, we measured a series of practical values of a variable (x′x'x​′​​), of which the distribution (g(x′)g(x')g(x​′​​)) was unknown. But we know the theoretic distribution of the variable (f(x)f(x)f(x)) and the correction relationship or mapping relationship between theoretic and practical values (x′=c(x)x'=c(x)x​′​​=c(x)). So we can get the analytic formula of the distribution g(x′)g(x')g(x​′​​) as following

∫x′∈R′g(x′)dx′=∫x∈Rf(x)dx′dxdx\int_{x' \in R'} g(x') dx' = \int_{x \in R} f(x) \frac{dx'}{dx} dx ∫​x​′​​∈R​′​​​​g(x​′​​)dx​′​​=∫​x∈R​​f(x)​dx​​dx​′​​​​dx

This is the one dimension situation of Jacobian determinant in integrals by substitution.

Cross products for parallelogram area

Let’s consider two vectors aT=[a1,a2],bT=[b1,b2]\mathbf{a}^T = [a_1,a_2], \mathbf{b}^T = [b_1, b_2]a​T​​=[a​1​​,a​2​​],b​T​​=[b​1​​,b​2​​]. We are very familar with the dot product or the inner product of the two vectors,

a⋅b=aTb=a1b1+a2b2\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{b} = a_1b_1+a_2b_2 a⋅b=a​T​​b=a​1​​b​1​​+a​2​​b​2​​

the name inner comes from the transpose symbol TTT inside the two vectors. The inner product can also be calculated as

a⋅b=∣a∣∣b∣cosθ\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta a⋅b=∣a∣∣b∣cosθ

The cross product of two vectors generally produces a new vector which is perpendicular to the plane consist of the two original vectors. If i,j,k\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}i,j,k are orthogonal unitary vectors, they satisify the following relationship via cross product,

i×j=k\mathbf{i} \times \mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k} i×j=k

j×k=i\mathbf{j} \times \mathbf{k} = \mathbf{i} j×k=i

k×i=j\mathbf{k} \times \mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j} k×i=j

So vectors a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b can be rewritten as a=a1i+a2j+0k\mathbf{a} = a_1\mathbf{i}+a_2\mathbf{j}+0\mathbf{k}a=a​1​​i+a​2​​j+0k and b=b1i+b2j+0k\mathbf{b} = b_1\mathbf{i}+b_2\mathbf{j}+0\mathbf{k}b=b​1​​i+b​2​​j+0k. Thus

a×b=(a1i+a2j+0k)×(b1i+b2j+0k)=(a1b2−a2b1)k=∣a1a2 b1b2∣k\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = (a_1\mathbf{i}+a_2\mathbf{j}+0\mathbf{k}) \times (b_1\mathbf{i}+b_2\mathbf{j}+0\mathbf{k}) = (a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\mathbf{k} = \begin{vmatrix} a_1&a_2 \\\ b_1&b_2 \end{vmatrix} \mathbf{k} a×b=(a​1​​i+a​2​​j+0k)×(b​1​​i+b​2​​j+0k)=(a​1​​b​2​​−a​2​​b​1​​)k=​∣​∣​∣​∣​​​a​1​​​ b​1​​​​​a​2​​​b​2​​​​​∣​∣​∣​∣​​k

The magnitude of the cross product of two vectors can also be calculated by

∣a×b∣=∣a∣∣b∣sinθ|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}| = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\sin\theta ∣a×b∣=∣a∣∣b∣sinθ

which is the area of the parallelogram having a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b as sides (0<θ<π0 < \theta < \pi0<θ<π).

Infinitesimal area in integration

For double integrals, we take an infinitesimal area as the integral element. Usually it was written as the direct product as dxdydxdydxdy because x-axis and y-axis are orthogonal (sinθ=1\sin\theta=1sinθ=1).

Now let us consider a function g(u,v)g(u,v)g(u,v) of variables uuu and vvv while u=u(x,y)u=u(x,y)u=u(x,y) and v=v(x,y)v=v(x,y)v=v(x,y). If we want to calculate the integral ∫g(u,v)dudv\int g(u,v) dudv∫g(u,v)dudv, we must firstly know the infinitesimal area dudvdudvdudv. Because u,vu, vu,v are functions of x,yx,yx,y, they have following relationship,

du=dudxdx+dudydydu = \frac{du}{dx}dx+\frac{du}{dy}dy du=​dx​​du​​dx+​dy​​du​​dy

dv=dvdxdx+dvdydydv = \frac{dv}{dx}dx+\frac{dv}{dy}dy dv=​dx​​dv​​dx+​dy​​dv​​dy

It’s analogous that vector u\mathbf{u}u has vector components dudxdx\frac{du}{dx}dx​dx​​du​​dx or dudydy\frac{du}{dy}dy​dy​​du​​dy on x-axis or y-axis direction respectively, and same for v\mathbf{v}v.

It’s a wrong way to get the infinitesimal area by multiplying dududu and dvdvdv directly. Because uuu and vvv are not guaranteed to be orthogonal. But we can get the infinitesimal area by calculating the cross product of dududu and dvdvdv,

du×dv=(uxvy−uyvx)dxdy=∣uxuy vxvy∣dxdy=∂(u,v)∂(x,y)dxdydu \times dv = (u_xv_y-u_yv_x)dxdy = \begin{vmatrix} u_x & u_y \\\ v_x & v_y \end{vmatrix} dxdy = \frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} dxdy du×dv=(u​x​​v​y​​−u​y​​v​x​​)dxdy=​∣​∣​∣​∣​​​u​x​​​ v​x​​​​​u​y​​​v​y​​​​​∣​∣​∣​∣​​dxdy=​∂(x,y)​​∂(u,v)​​dxdy

∂(u,v)∂(x,y)\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}​∂(x,y)​​∂(u,v)​​ is called the Jacobian determinant. It can be seen as the chage ratio of area (or volume etc.) after variables mapping.
